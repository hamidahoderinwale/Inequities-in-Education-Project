Analyzing Gender and Social Imbalances in the Canadian Education System




Analyzing the Proportion of male and female postsecondary enrolments, by field of study and International Standard Classification of Education.

Analysis done by Eric Y, Visualization done by Hamidah

These graphs compare the proportion of males and females (aged 20-24), post-secondary enrollments in various bachelor's (or equivalent programs) across Canada between 1997-1998 and 2018-2019. This age group was selected as it represents a formative and accurate predictor for future career paths and is when the majority of the population applies to post-secondary programs. Male-dominated fields of study (defined as >70% male) have not changed across the years, remaining as Mathematics, Information, and Computer Science (73.1% between 1997-1998 and 72.6% between 2018-2019) and Architecture, Engineering, and Related Technologies (78.2% between 1997-1998 and 75.4% between 2018-2019). The minor decreases in male enrollment (implying an increase in female enrollment) in these fields could be attributed to the changing attitudes towards females in higher education in the 1990s (1). Female-dominated fields of study (defined as >70% female), however, have undergone changes. For instance, between 1997 and 2019, Health and related fields became female-dominated with a change from 69.3% female to 75.4% female, respectively. One female-dominated that has remained consistent is Education (78.8% between 1997-1998 and 79.3% between 2018-2019). Additionally, no data was collected for Personal Improvement and Leisure between 1997-1998 although it was shown to be a female-dominated field between 2018-2019 with 70.4% female enrollment.

Analyzing the Rising Costs of Tuition Between the Years of 2017 and 2021.

Analysis done by Linda Duong, Vizualizations done by Hamidah

The graph reveals several significant trends in Canadian university tuition fees. Although the fees do not fluctuate significantly by year, veterinary medicine has the most significant change in price from 2017 to 2022, with an increase of $6,727. In general, the most expensive undergraduate major tended to be associated with healthcare, with dentistry ($22,731), medicine ($14,604), veterinary medicine ($14,374), and optometry ($10,735) in the 2021-2022 school year. The most expensive non-medicine degree was law, which is $12,910 in the 2021-2022 year.

The expensive cost of these majors might provide a barrier to those degrees specifically, especially as medicine and law usually lead to a higher degree, like a Master's. However, the average tuition cost for all degrees was $6,618 in 2017-2018 to $6,693 in 2021-2022. Thus, the average tuition increased by $75 over the past 5 years., which is not too much. Various degrees associated with a high-paying career, like engineering ($8,184) and math/computer science ($6,953), are not as expensive. As a whole, the tuition fees are not prohibitively expensive to Canadians. It is seen that minority groups including women, black and indigenous people in particular suffer at the hands of a system that leads to lower incomes and thus a lack of educational attainability due to rising costs, disproportionately leads to lower economic growth in these groups.



"Characteristics and median employment income of longitudinal cohorts of postsecondary graduates two and five years after graduation, by educational qualification and field of study (STEM and BHASE (non-STEM) groupings)"

Analysis and Visualizations done by Hamidah Oderinwale (done in Matplotlib and Numpy)

These graphs reveal the changes in annual income for both females and males who have graduated in STEM and BHASE fields. For each year, from 2010 to 2014, there is no evidence that wages have significantly changed during the timeframe, however, it is apparent that although the BHASE incomes tend to be lower overall, Females have also seemed to make less than their male counterparts, upon completing post-secondary education in both fields, with a greater disparity being seen in STEM wages. These insights are especially important as oftentimes, Canada and its workforce are portrayed as liberal, supporting equality and employee satisfaction have driven, when the data shows otherwise, representing antiquated ideals over the concept of women-in-the-workplace.
